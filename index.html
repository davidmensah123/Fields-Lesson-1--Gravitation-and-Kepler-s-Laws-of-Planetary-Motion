Nature of Science

"In this world, there is a natural cause for things to happen. For example, if a ball falls to the ground, we assume it is the result of gravity. Newton's great intellectual leap was to realize that the same force pulling an apple to the ground also keeps the Moon orbiting the Earth. By unifying terrestrial and astronomical physics, Newton transformed observational patterns (Kepler's Laws) into a single predictive model of Universal Gravitation, which was later modified by Einstein's space-time curvature."

1. Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

Newton's Law of Gravitation states that any two point masses in the universe attract each other with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers: $$F = G \frac{Mm}{r^2}$$ where \(G \approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2\text{ kg}^{-2}\) is the universal gravitational constant. This force acts along the line joining the centers of the two masses and represents an attractive action-reaction pair matching Newton's Third Law.

2. Gravitational Field Inside a Planet

For a point outside a spherical planet of radius \(R\), the gravitational field strength acts as though all planetary mass \(M\) is concentrated at its center: $$g_{\text{outside}} = \frac{GM}{r^2} \quad (r \ge R)$$ However, for a point *inside* a uniform density planet (\(r < R\)), shell theorem dictates that only the sphere of mass inside radius \(r\) exerts a net gravitational pull. The outer shell's forces cancel out. $$\text{Mass inside } r: \quad m = M \left(\frac{r^3}{R^3}\right)$$ $$\text{Field inside } r: \quad g_{\text{inside}} = \frac{G m}{r^2} = \frac{G M r}{R^3} \quad (r < R)$$ Therefore, inside a uniform planet, gravity is **directly proportional to distance from the center** (\(g \propto r\)), peaking on the surface before decaying exponentially outside (\(g \propto \frac{1}{r^2}\)).

3. Newton's Cannonball Thought Experiment

Isaac Newton imagined a cannon fired horizontally from a high mountain. At low speeds, the projectile falls back to Earth. As the launch velocity increases, the curvature of the trajectory matches the curvature of the Earth. At this point, the projectile is in continuous free-fall, orbiting the planet: $$F_{\text{centripetal}} = F_{\text{gravity}} \implies \frac{m v^2}{r} = G \frac{Mm}{r^2} \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

5. Mathematical Derivations

Derivation: Kepler's Third Law (\(T^2 \propto r^3\))

Step 1: Equate Gravitational Attraction to Centripetal Force
For a planet of mass \(m\) orbiting a star of mass \(M\) in a stable circular orbit of radius \(r\) at velocity \(v\):
\[\frac{G M m}{r^2} = \frac{m v^2}{r} \implies v^2 = \frac{G M}{r}\]

Step 2: Express velocity in terms of period \(T\)
Circular velocity is defined by the circumference divided by the orbital period: \(v = \frac{2\pi r}{T}\). Substitute this expression into the velocity equation:
\[\left(\frac{2\pi r}{T}\right)^2 = \frac{G M}{r} \implies \frac{4\pi^2 r^2}{T^2} = \frac{G M}{r}\]

Step 3: Isolate period \(T^2\)
Multiply both sides by \(r\) and solve for \(T^2\):
\[T^2 = \left(\frac{4\pi^2}{G M}\right) r^3 \implies \mathbf{T^2 \propto r^3}\]

6. Solved Worked Examples

Worked Example 1: Earth-Moon Gravitational Pull

Question: Calculate the mutual gravitational force of attraction between the Earth and the Moon.
• Earth mass \(M_E \approx 5.97 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}\)
• Moon mass \(M_M \approx 7.35 \times 10^{22}\text{ kg}\)
• Average separation distance \(r \approx 3.84 \times 10^{8}\text{ m}\)

Step-by-step Solution:

We apply Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation:
\[F = G \frac{M_E M_M}{r^2}\]
Substitute the physical constants:
\[F = (6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \frac{(5.97 \times 10^{24}) \times (7.35 \times 10^{22})}{(3.84 \times 10^{8})^2}\]
Evaluate the numerator and denominator:
\[F = (6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \frac{4.38795 \times 10^{47}}{1.47456 \times 10^{17}} = (6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (2.9757 \times 10^{30}) \approx \mathbf{1.98 \times 10^{20}\text{ N}}\]

Worked Example 2: Determining Martian Mass

Question: Mars' moon Phobos orbits Mars with a radius of \(r = 9.38 \times 10^6\text{ m}\) and an orbital period of \(T = 7.66\text{ hours}\). Deducing from Kepler's Third Law, calculate the mass of Mars.

Step-by-step Solution:

Step 1: Convert period \(T\) to seconds
\[T = 7.66\text{ hours} \times 3600\text{ s/hour} = 27,576\text{ s}\]

Step 2: Apply Kepler's Third Law
\[T^2 = \left(\frac{4\pi^2}{GM}\right)r^3 \implies M = \frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{G T^2}\]
Substitute Mars parameters:
\[M = \frac{4 \pi^2 \times (9.38 \times 10^6)^3}{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times (27576)^2} = \frac{4 \pi^2 \times (8.253 \times 10^{20})}{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 7.604 \times 10^8}\]
\[M = \frac{3.258 \times 10^{22}}{5.072 \times 10^{-2}} \approx \mathbf{6.42 \times 10^{23}\text{ kg}}\]

Calculation Challenges

Challenge 1: Geostationary Orbit Altitude

[7 Marks]

A geostationary satellite remains stationary relative to a point on Earth's equator by revolving with a period equal to Earth's sidereal rotation (\(T = 86,164\text{ s}\)). Take Earth's mass as \(M_E = 5.97 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}\) and \(G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11}\text{ N m}^2\text{ kg}^{-2}\).

a) Calculate the orbital radius \(r\) of a geostationary satellite. [4 marks]

meters (m)

b) Calculate the linear orbital speed \(v\) of the geostationary satellite. [3 marks]

m/s

Step-by-step Solution:

Part a) Orbital Radius:
From Kepler's Third Law:
\[r^3 = \frac{G M_E T^2}{4\pi^2}\]
Substitute constants:
\[r^3 = \frac{(6.67 \times 10^{-11}) \times (5.97 \times 10^{24}) \times (86164)^2}{4\pi^2} = \frac{2.956 \times 10^{24}}{39.478} \approx 7.487 \times 10^{22}\text{ m}^3\]
Take the cube root:
\[r = \sqrt[3]{7.487 \times 10^{22}} \approx \mathbf{4.21 \times 10^7\text{ m}} \quad (\approx 42,100\text{ km})\]

Part b) Linear Speed:
Using the circular velocity relationship:
\[v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} = \frac{2\pi \times 4.215 \times 10^7}{86164} \approx \mathbf{3070\text{ m/s}} \quad (\approx 3.07\text{ km/s})\]

Challenge 2: Piecewise Field Strengths

[6 Marks]

A hypothetical planet with a perfectly uniform density has a mass of \(M = 3.00 \times 10^{24}\text{ kg}\) and a radius of \(R = 5,000\text{ km}\) (\(5.00 \times 10^6\text{ m}\)).

a) Calculate the gravitational field strength \(g\) on the surface of this planet. [3 marks]

m/s²

b) Calculate the field strength \(g\) at a point halfway down to the center (\(r = 2,500\text{ km}\)). [3 marks]

m/s²

Mathematical Proof & Explanation:

Part a) Surface Gravity:
Using the standard inverse-square law for points outside/on the surface:
\[g = \frac{G M}{R^2} = \frac{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 3.00 \times 10^{24}}{(5.00 \times 10^6)^2} = \frac{2.001 \times 10^{14}}{2.50 \times 10^{13}} = \mathbf{8.00\text{ m/s}^2}\]

Part b) Internal Gravity halfway down:
Inside a uniform density sphere, gravity decreases linearly with distance to the center: \(g_{\text{inside}} = g_{\text{surf}} \left(\frac{r}{R}\right)\). At \(r = 0.5R\):
\[g = 8.00 \times \left(\frac{2.50 \times 10^6}{5.00 \times 10^6}\right) = 8.00 \times 0.5 = \mathbf{4.00\text{ m/s}^2}\]

Challenge 3: Binary Star System Orbit

[6 Marks]

Two identical stars of equal mass \(M = 3.00 \times 10^{30}\text{ kg}\) are in circular orbits of radius \(R = 6.50 \times 10^{8}\text{ m}\) around their common center of mass (the distance separating their centers is \(2R\)). Take \(G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11}\text{ N m}^2\text{ kg}^{-2}\).

a) Calculate the orbital speed \(v\) of each star in \(\text{km s}^{-1}\). [3 marks]

km/s

b) Calculate the orbital period \(T\) of this system in hours. [3 marks]

hours

Step-by-step Solution:

Part a) Orbital Speed:
The gravitational force acting between the two stars of separation \(2R\) provides the centripetal force for each star orbiting at radius \(R\):
\[\frac{G M^2}{(2R)^2} = \frac{M v^2}{R} \implies \frac{G M}{4R^2} = \frac{v^2}{R} \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{G M}{4R}}\]
Substitute the values:
\[v = \sqrt{\frac{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times 3.00 \times 10^{30}}{4 \times 6.50 \times 10^8}} = \sqrt{\frac{2.001 \times 10^{20}}{2.60 \times 10^9}} = \sqrt{7.696 \times 10^{10}} \approx 2.774 \times 10^5\text{ m/s}\]
Converting to kilometers per second:
\[v \approx \mathbf{277\text{ km/s}}\] (or \(277.4\text{ km/s}\))

Part b) Orbital Period:
Using the orbital relationship \(v = \frac{2\pi R}{T}\), we isolate \(T\):
\[T = \frac{2\pi R}{v} = \frac{2\pi \times 6.50 \times 10^8}{2.774 \times 10^5} \approx 14725\text{ s}\]
Converting to hours:
\[T = \frac{14725}{3600} \approx \mathbf{4.1\text{ hours}}\] (or \(4.09\text{ hours}\))
